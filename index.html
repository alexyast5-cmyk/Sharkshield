Our Solution

SharkShield — a wearable electric field that surrounds the swimmer.

Waist and ankle straps pulse 75V through the surrounding seawater, exceeding the scientifically established shark deterrence threshold at approximately 1 metre from the device. One button. Strap on. Jump in.

⚡
Electric field — not a repellent
Sharks detect prey through the ampullae of Lorenzini — electrosensory pores on their snout sensitive to 5 nV/cm. SharkShield overwhelms this sense with a field exceeding the peer-reviewed deterrence threshold, causing the shark to turn away.
🌊
The ocean completes the circuit
Waist electrodes (positive) and ankle electrodes (negative) form a dipole. Seawater between them carries the field outward in all directions around the swimmer. No wires between straps — the ocean is the conductor.
○
360° coverage, no blind spots
Body-fixed bilateral electrode geometry means the field surrounds the swimmer on all sides. Unlike trailing antenna designs, there is no direction a shark can approach from without encountering the field.
◈
Validated by peer-reviewed science
The 3 V/m bull shark deterrence threshold is established by Huveneers et al. (Griffith University, 2013) — the same research behind Ocean Guardian. COMSOL simulation confirms SharkShield exceeds this at ~1m.
How It Works

The science behind the field.

01
Capacitor charges to 75V
A boost converter steps up the LiPo battery voltage to 75V and charges a small capacitor. This happens in microseconds between each pulse.
02
5 microsecond pulse fires through seawater
The capacitor discharges in 5 microseconds through the titanium electrodes. 75V propagates outward through seawater in all directions around the swimmer at 2–10 Hz.
03
Field exceeds deterrence threshold at ~1m
COMSOL electromagnetic simulation confirms field strength of 3+ V/m at approximately 1m from the waist electrode — the peer-reviewed bull shark aversion threshold. Great white threshold (7–9 V/m) exceeded within 0.6m.
04
Shark turns away
At this field strength, sharks exhibit documented head-twitching and retreat. In the terminal strike phase (<0.5m), sharks close their eyes and navigate solely via electroreception — at 30+ V/m this sense is completely overwhelmed.

Why It's Safe

High field in water.
Safe for the person wearing it.

V/m (field strength in water) and current through tissue are different physical quantities. Here is the definitive safety case — including absolute worst case.

Worst case calculation: all 75V drives directly through the body, no seawater path, no backplate. Body resistance: 500Ω. Current: 150mA. Pulse: 5µs.

Charge delivered = 0.15A × 0.000005s = 0.00075 mC
IEC 60479-2 cardiac safety limit = 3 mC
Safety margin = 4,000× below the limit.

This is not a marginal result. Even at 0.1ms pulses the margin is 200×. The device is safe by physics, not by assumption.

Seawater carries 96% of the current
Seawater conductivity is 4 S/m. Body tissue is 0.2 S/m — 20× more resistive. Current follows the path of least resistance. ~96% routes through seawater, ~4% through the body. The ocean itself protects the swimmer.
Insulated backplate blocks body contact
Each electrode has a silicone insulating backplate on the skin-facing side. Current is physically forced outward into seawater. If the electrode leaves the water, the circuit opens completely — zero current flows.
5µs pulses — below sensory threshold
Published strength-duration curves confirm at 5µs pulse duration, 75V is below the human sensory threshold. The wearer feels nothing — even in the absolute worst case where all current passes through the body.
Patent precedent confirms the architecture
US Patent 8,988,226 specifies exactly this design: alternating-polarity pulses, ~10µs width, 150–200V for body-mounted shark deterrents. Our 75V at 5µs is more conservative than the patented specification.
